By Krystal Won

Picture this: you're at a wild party, the dance floor's packed and the energy's off the charts. Suddenly, the music drops out. Half the crowd's gone. Then more leave. And more. Until it's just you, swaying awkwardly all alone. Sounds pretty dreadful, right? Well, that's kind of what's happening to our planet -- we're losing species.

Humans trying to save endangered species is like running a marathon where the finish line keeps moving further away. We're running, we're sweating, we're putting in the effort, but no matter how hard we try, it never seems enough to get us across the line. The dual threats of overexploitation and climatic upheaval aren't just moving the goalposts but turning the whole field into quicksand.

Source: Earth's Endangered Creatures

1. African Forest Elephant: Gabon's Green Heart Beats with a Fading Pulse

The verdant heart of Central Africa, Gabon, is home to a unique dweller –- the African forest elephant. In 2021, the reality of this majestic creature's plight was revealed through a solemn expedition by dedicated researchers. The revelations were stark - the population had dwindled to approximately 95,000. Over the span of 31 years, a devastating decline of over 86% had occurred. Classified as critically endangered by the IUCN in the same year, this singular species, one of two African elephant types, grapples with survival in their ever-constricting forest homes.

The Dark Side of Demand - Poaching and the Threat to African Forest Elephants

The primary threats to these elephants stem from rampant poaching, driven by the demand for bushmeat and the insidious global ivory trade. Thousands of these grand creatures are killed every year to feed this illegal demand. The magnitude of this tragedy was gruesomely illustrated in January 2012, when a horde of Sudanese poachers massacred over 200 elephants in a solitary national park in Cameroon.

2. The Amur Leopard: A Struggle Against Extinction

The Amur leopard, known as the world's rarest big cat, was first classified as critically endangered by IUCN back in 1960. This leopard subspecies, one among the eight that inhabit the terrains of Africa and Asia, has been teetering on the brink of extinction for decades. In 2007, estimates suggested as few as 19 to 26 wild leopards left. By 2015, the count barely crossed 60 in both Russia and China.

Recovery on the Horizon

However, recent reports from 2021 indicate a potential turning point. Current estimates put the wild Amur leopard population at around 110. These numbers represent not just a glimmer of hope, but the fruit of concerted conservation efforts.

Let’s go behind the numbers and swing back to 1999. Back then, the Russian government had approved a plan for the protection of the Amur leopard. Furthermore, Russian President Vladimir Putin, in power since 2000, showed his commitment to the preservation of this species by signing an order in 2012. This order expanded the leopard's protected habitat and led to the establishment of the 269,000-hectare "Land of the Leopard '' national park.

3. For Eastern Lowland Gorilla, It's a Desperate Battle Against a 58% Population Decline

In the heart of the rainforests of the eastern Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) roams one of the world's most endangered primates, the eastern lowland gorilla.

Previous years' study pointed out that fewer than 4,000 of these remarkable creatures are estimated to inhabit the wild, but the story is evolving. Fortunately, a revised density estimate now puts the population at around 6,800 - a flicker of hope, yet far from a sigh of relief.

Over the span of a single generation, from 1995 to 2016, the population of these gorillas plunged by a staggering 58%, dwindling at an annual rate of 4.0%. To put it in perspective, the current population of 5,252 is just 42% of the 1995-1996 estimate of 12,390 for the same area. The drastic decline led to the classification of the Grauer's gorilla as Critically Endangered in 2016.

The Biological Bond and Risks Between Humans and Gorillas

What's equally striking is our biological kinship with these animals. Human share around 98% of our DNA with gorillas, marking them as our closest relatives after chimpanzees and bonobos. This close genetic link, however, leaves them vulnerable to human diseases, even common colds, as they haven't developed necessary immunities or any medical protection. Their diet, mainly composed of stems, bamboo shoots, and fruits, is indicative of their gentle, non-aggressive nature.

Poaching, Disease, and Tourism's Complex Impact on Gorilla Survival

However, they face an array of threats, primarily stemming from human activities. Poaching, human-related diseases like Ebola, and the destruction of their native afromontane forest home all pose considerable challenges.

In a poignant incident in 2007, an entire family of mountain gorillas was slaughtered by locals, a political act aimed at forcing the park open for resource exploitation.

Even tourism, despite its benefits in bring up economy status for conservation, adds to the threats. Although visitors are instructed to maintain a distance of 7 meters, the presence of conservationists, scientists, rangers, and even local communities in their habitat adds layers of complexity to their survival.

4. Javan Rhinos: The Most Tenuous Existence Among Its Family

In a remote corner of Ujung Kulon National Park in Java, Indonesia, a rare and precious population of Javan rhinos clings to survival. With just 76 individuals remaining on our planets, they are the most endangered among all rhino species. Once thriving across the lush landscapes of northeast India and Southeast Asia, their range has been decimated, leaving only this small enclave as their last stronghold.

This extraordinary species, the Javan rhino, is confined to a single protected area, making their survival all the more precarious. Their biology remains shrouded in mystery, as scientists have struggled to gain comprehensive knowledge about their behaviors and needs. The dense jungles they inhabit have hindered detailed observation, and the species has never successfully bred in captivity, further exacerbating their vulnerability.

Escalating Threats

Recent years have brought fresh challenges to the dwindling Javan rhino population. Disease has emerged as a deadly threat, claiming the lives of these rare rhinos. The root of the disease can be traced back to wild cattle in the park, underscoring the complex web of interactions within the ecosystem and the grave consequences they can have for these magnificent creatures.

Sadly, the last hope for wild Javan rhinos outside Ujung Kulon National Park vanished in 2010. A rhino in Cat Tien National Park, Vietnam, fell victim to poaching, its horn savagely removed. This cruel act sealed the fate of the species in Vietnam, officially declaring it extinct the following year.

5. The Precarious Journey of Black Rhinos: Battling Decline and Illegal Trade

Black rhinos, adorned with their distinctive gray coats, possess two magnificent horns, with the foremost horn prominently longer than the other. Their adaptability is noteworthy, as they can thrive in a wide array of habitats, ranging from arid deserts in Namibia to verdant woodlands offering respite and sustenance.

Once roaming throughout the vast landscapes of sub-Saharan Africa, the black rhino population witnessed a remarkable heyday in the late 1800s and early 1900s, thriving with an estimated count of up to 850,000 individuals. However, the passage of time brought profound challenges, and by 1970, their numbers had dwindled to a mere 65,000, scattered across isolated pockets of their once abundant habitat.

This marked the beginning of a downward spiral for the species. Large-scale poaching activities fueled by the illegal international trade in rhino horn became a menacing force, driving the black rhino population to the brink of oblivion.

By 1995, their situation had reached a critical point, with a devastating decline of 98% since 1960, leaving only 2,410 individuals remaining. The relentless demand for rhino horn, highly prized in traditional Chinese medicine and for ornamental purposes, pushed these magnificent creatures to the edge of extinction.
